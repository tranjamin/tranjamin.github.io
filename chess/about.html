Customising your Game

1. Name your chess server

This is almost completely unrestricted

2. Choose what colour to play with

Randomly chosen games are labelled as such for different users

3. Choose a game mode

Explore the different chess variants in depth in the next tab

4. Choose your visibility

Public games are open to anyone, and are searchable. Private games require the exact name to find

5. Invite a user

You can enter the name of a user, who will get a notification to join your game. However, this does not automatically set the opponent as the user

6. Mode

Choose whether the game is casual or rated (affects your elo rating). Any rated game to which an anonymous user participates in is automatically changed to casual

7. Choose your time control

Time is automatically set as limited, but standardised options or a custom time control can be formed

Chess Variants

Classic

If classic chess was a spice, it would be flour

Chess960 (Fischer Random Chess)

In Chess960, the starting positions are randomised, creating 960 possible combinations

  1. White's 8 back-rank pieces are randomly placed on the first rank
  2. Black's pieces are placed to mirror White's
  3. The bishops must be on opposing colours
  4. The king must be between the two rooks
  5. In castling, the king and rook end up on the same squares as in classic chess

Antichess

In Antichess, your aim is to lose all of your pieces, but capturing is compulsory.

  1. If you can capture an opposing piece, you must do so.
  2. If you can capture multiple pieces, you can decide which one to capture.
  3. There is no check or checkmate; the king can be captured like any other piece.
  4. As such, pawns can promote to kings
  5. There is no castling
  6. A player wins if they have no legal moves possible (if there is a stalemate or they have no pieces remaining)
  7. The game is drawn if neither player can capture the other player's pieces (ie. two opposite-coloured bishops)

Armageddon

In Armageddon, there is always a decisive victory.

  1. In drawn games, Black always wins
  2. To compensate, White is given time odds; in this game, they are given 20% more time.
  3. All other rules are the same as Classical Chess

Atomic Chess

Pieces "explode" when captured.

  1. When a piece is captured, both the capturing and captured piece are removed
  2. Additionally, all non-pawn pieces in the surrounding 8 squares are removed
  3. A player cannot capture a piece that would result in their own king being exploded; as such, the king can never capture a piece
  4. There is no check, so two kings can occupy adjacent spaces
  5. A player wins by exploding the opposing king without exploding their own
  6. A player also wins if the opposing king has no legal moves and is in direct check, if not, the game is a stalemate

Beirut Chess

One piece each, unbeknowngst to the other player, is equipped with a suicide vest.

  1. On their turn, instead of playing a regular turn, the designated piece can be detonated
  2. This explosion kills the bomb carrier as well as any piece on the surrounding 8 squares, regardless of colour
  3. When a piece is captured, the piece cannot be inspected to see if it carried the bomb
  4. A player wins by checkmate, resignation, or by exploding the king

Crazyhouse

A captured piece can be introduced as the player's own

  1. On their turn, instead of playing a regular turn, any captured piece can be dropped onto an empty space on the board
  2. This piece changes colour from the opposing colour to the player's own
  3. This piece can be dropped to avoid a certain checkmate, or to create a checkmate
  4. When captured, promoted pawns are kept as pawns and not queens
  5. Pawns cannot be dropped on the 1st rank (for Black) or 8th rank (for White)
  6. Pawns dropped on the 2nd rank (for White) or 7th rank (for Black) can be moved two squares on their first move, as usual
  7. Dropped rooks are not available for castling
  8. Drop moves are denoted with the @ symbol (eg. N@b5)

3 Check Checkmate

A player can win if they put their opponent in check 3 times

Checkless Chess

Putting the opponent in check is illegal unless it is checkmate

  1. A move cannot be made if it puts the enemy king in check without checkmating it
  2. As such, the king becomes very powerful

Circe Chess

Instead of pieces being removed when captured, they are reborn

  1. Any captured piece returns to its starting square, with the rooks, bishops and knights returning to the starting square the same colour as the square they were captured on
  2. Pawns are returned on the file they were captured on
  3. Reborn pawns are able to move two squares on their first move
  4. Reborn rooks can be castled with
  5. Any captured promoted pawns are treated as a piece, not a pawn
  6. If the starting square is occupied, the piece is removed instead of reborn
  7. A rebirth is denoted with parantheses after the move (eg. Rxe5(Bc3))

Dusanny's Chess

White's horde of pawns take on Black's army

  1. Black's starting position is conventional, whilst White starts with 32 pawns
  2. Black starts
  3. Only Black's pawns are able to move two squares on their first move
  4. White wins by checkmating Black, whilst Black wins by capturing all of White's pieces

King of the Hill

A player can also win by legally moving their king to one of the center squares (d4,d5,e4,e5)

Really Bad Chess

This variation is really really bad

  1. White and Black's pieces are completely randomised and not identical
  2. The Kings start in their regular positions, but for the remaining 15 squares a piece is chosen at random
  3. Due to the likelihood that one player will have a large advantage, these games are not rated

Schrodinger Chess

This version is waaaaaay to complicated to explain, but I'll try anyway

  1. A player can choose to make a quantum or regular move
  2. If they choose a quantum move, they may move a piece twice, but there is a 50% chance that the move did not occur
  3. As a result of this uncertainty, there are two versions of the piece, and either can be moved
  4. When a piece is captured, the wave function collapses and, based on probability, it is determined if the piece is actually in that location. This can result in a chain of collapses
  5. As such, there is no check or checkmate unless there is a 100% chance that the king is in that location, otherwise, the king has to be captured to determine if the game is over or not
  6. Rwo pieces can become entangled if one moves through a square that the other one has a probability of occuring at. The probability that the first piece has moved is equal to the probability that the second piece is not in the sqaure

How to format Time Control

Interpreting time control

Time control is split into phases (separated by spaces). In each phase, a number of turns (G for the rest of the game) is allocated a number of minutes in which it must be completed, with any time remaining from the previous phase carried over. An increment may also be added, a time in seconds which is added each turn.

For example, in the time control (40/90+30 + G/30+30), 90 minutes is first added to the clock, and after 40 turns another 30 minutes is added. For every move throughout the game, 30 seconds is added after its completion.

Standardised time control formats

FIDE International Time (40/90+30 G/30+30): 90m for the first 40 moves, followed by 30m for the rest of the game, with a 30s interval

FIDE Candidates Tournament / Tata Steel (40/100+30 20/50+30 G/15+30): 100m for the first 40 moves, followed by 50m for the next 20 and 15m for the rest of the game, with a 30s interval

Rapid Time (G/25): 25m for the entire game, with no interval

Blitz Time (G/5): 5m for the entire game, with no interval

Bullet Time (G/1): 1m for the entire game, with no interval
